Baseline · applies to all 14 shipments

The customs foundation

Required for any commercial import from China, structure or kit alike. Set these up once, before the first purchase order.

CRA · CBSA (CARM)

Importer registration & CARM

Hold a CRA Business Number with an import–export (RM) account, and register in the CARM Client Portal — the official system of record since Oct 21, 2024. To get goods released before paying (Release Prior to Payment), you must now post your own financial security (bond or cash) — you can no longer ride on a broker’s bond.

Applies to: every listing · new 2024–25 obligation
CBSA · Dept. of Finance

GST, duty & HS classification

5% GST is collected at the border on the value for duty (Ontario’s 8% isn’t collected at import — you self-account and usually recover it as an input tax credit). Every item gets a 10-digit HS classification that sets its duty rate; China gets MFN treatment (many of these goods are MFN-free). Value for duty is the transaction value.

Applies to: every listing · classification drives the surtax exposure below
CFIA

Wood packaging — ISPM 15

All wood crates, pallets, dunnage and bracing from China must be heat-treated and stamped with the IPPC mark. Non-compliant wood is a leading cause of held containers — CFIA can order the shipment treated, re-exported or destroyed at your expense. The glulam A-Frame and any wood components need a plant-health check of their own.

Applies to: every crated shipment
CBSA

Origin marking & documents

Goods must be marked “Made in China” — legible and permanent — to reach the ultimate purchaser. Every shipment needs a commercial invoice, packing list and bill of lading. Unmarked goods can be held and draw monetary (AMPS) penalties. A licensed customs broker is optional but standard for first-time importers.

Applies to: every listing
Track A · prefab structures

What the 10 buildings add

These are steel, aluminium and glass — the exact materials Canada now surtaxes from China. The tariff classification of your kit is a five-figure decision.

Dept. of Finance · CBSA

25% surtax on Chinese steel & aluminium

Since Oct 22, 2024, listed steel (Ch. 72–73) and aluminium (Ch. 76) goods from China carry a 25% surtax. The hinge: a complete prefab building classifies under heading 9406 (outside the list), but bare steel/aluminium structural parts fall under 7308/7610 (on it). Same crate, very different landed cost. Confirm the exact tariff item with a broker (Customs Notice CN24-36).

Applies to: all-steel & aluminium units — Chun Shu, Steel Modular, capsules, Branson, glass/aluminium domes
Electrical Safety Authority (ON)

Fitted electrical → ESA approval

Turnkey capsules and villas ship with wiring, lighting, heating and “smart” systems. Under the Ontario Electrical Safety Code, that equipment can’t be connected until it’s approved — a recognized mark (cULus/CSA) or an SPE-1000 field evaluation. Chinese-standard voltage (e.g. 220 V) also needs conversion to 120/240 V.

Applies to: Standing Glass Dome, Chun Shu, Steel Modular, Steele T5/T9, ONE, Two Floor + optional bath/kitchen modules
ISED Canada

Wireless “smart” modules → ISED

The smart-capsule listings advertise WiFi/app-controlled systems. Any RF/wireless device must be ISED-certified (e.g. RSS-247) and labelled with its certification number, and the importer is responsible for ensuring it. RSS-247 Issue 4 took effect July 24, 2025 — use current supplier test reports.

Applies to: Steele T5/T9, ONE, Two Floor and any smart-controlled unit
Natural Resources Canada

Packaged appliances → NRCan

The core building isn’t energy-regulated, but a fridge, AC, water heater or dryer shipped inside a turnkey capsule is a regulated product — the importer must meet the efficiency standard and file energy-efficiency data. (Standalone solar inverters, batteries and turbines are not on NRCan’s list.)

Applies to: turnkey units with built-in appliances (Steele T9, ONE, etc.)
Track B · off-grid electrical

What the 4 power kits add

Two hard gates dominate: getting uncertified gear legally approved for connection, and shipping lithium batteries as dangerous goods.

Electrical Safety Authority (ON)

Electrical approval or SPE-1000 field evaluation

The single biggest gate. No electrical equipment may be sold or connected in Ontario unless approved: either it carries a Canada-recognized mark (cULus / CSA — a Chinese-only or bare-US “UL” mark isn’t enough), or it passes a CSA SPE-1000 field evaluation per unit/model. Budget cost and lead time for field evaluation on any uncertified inverter, battery or turbine.

Applies to: all 6 kits — inverters, controllers, batteries, panels & turbines
IMDG (sea) · Transport Canada TDG (road)

Lithium batteries as dangerous goods

LiFePO4 packs are Class 9 dangerous goods — UN 3480 alone, UN 3481 with equipment. Sea freight must meet the IMDG Code (packing, labels, signed DG declaration); the port-to-Manitoulin truck leg falls under Canada’s TDG regs. Every model needs a UN 38.3 test summary + SDS. The Chinese shipper/consignor carries primary legal responsibility — require compliant documentation.

Applies to: battery kits — Solar Power System, Full Solar + Wind Kit, HuiJue
Dept. of Finance Canada

Solar & battery surtax — on the watchlist

As of mid-2025, Chinese solar products and batteries are not surtaxed — but Finance Canada ran a consultation on exactly these categories (solar, batteries, critical minerals). A future order would hit your Track B goods directly. Re-verify the current status before placing any solar or battery order.

Applies to: all solar & battery kits
ISED Canada

Wireless monitors → ISED

WiFi/Bluetooth inverter and battery monitors are RF devices and must be ISED-certified and labelled. Cabling, panels and passive BOS aren’t, but the “smart” monitoring most of these kits include is. The importer/distributor is on the hook for compliance.

Applies to: kits with app/WiFi monitoring
Before you buy

Two decisions to nail, one document set to demand

Almost every money-critical surprise on this list traces back to two unknowns and a stack of supplier paperwork.

The two decisions that move money

Settle both before signing a purchase order.

  1. 1Exact HS classification of each kit. Whole building (9406, likely surtax-free) vs. steel/aluminium parts (7308/7610, +25%). Get a licensed broker’s written classification — it’s a five-figure swing per unit.
  2. 2Certification status of every electrical device. A Canada-recognized mark (cULus/CSA) clears ESA instantly; anything uncertified needs an SPE-1000 field evaluation — price the cost and lead time per model before you commit.

Documents to require from the supplier

Written into the purchase contract, not chased after loading.

  • Commercial invoice, packing list & bill of lading
  • ISPM 15 heat-treatment certificate for all wood packaging
  • UN 38.3 test summary + SDS and a signed DG declaration for every battery model
  • Certification marks / test reports for inverters, batteries, turbines (or agreement to field-evaluate)
  • ISED certification numbers for any wireless modules
  • “Made in China” origin marking on goods & cartons
Recently changed — re-verify at order time: CARM financial security (importers must now post their own bond/deposit for Release Prior to Payment) · China steel & aluminium 25% surtax (live since Oct 22, 2024; exposure turns on the 9406-vs-7308/7610 classification — confirm against Customs Notice CN24-36) · Solar & battery surtax (consulted on but not imposed as of mid-2025; the most likely near-term change to hit Track B) · ISED RSS-247 Issue 4 (July 24, 2025) and NRCan Amendment 19 (2025–27) — use current supplier test reports.
